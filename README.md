## The Three Pillars

My worldview rests on three interlocking foundations:

### 1. Torah — The Moral Foundation
Every human being is created *b'tzelem Elohim* — in the image of God. This is not poetry. It is a claim with radical implications: every person carries inherent worth, every life has purpose, and no institution has the right to diminish either. The Abrahamic prohibition on dishonest measures (*moznei tzedek*) is not merely a commercial rule — it is a statement about the nature of reality. Honest weights and measures are how a society expresses its belief in human dignity.

### 2. Adam Smith — The Economic Framework
Markets work because they harness self-interest in service of mutual benefit. But Smith's deeper insight — the one his modern admirers quietly forget — is that markets must be embedded in morality. The wise man sacrifices private interest to public good. The dignity of the butcher, the brewer, and the baker matters more than the comfort of the aristocrat. Concentrated power corrupts markets as surely as it corrupts governments. Smith was not a libertarian. He was a moralist who understood economics.

### 3. Bitcoin — The Honest Measure
Fiat money is the modern dishonest weight. It allows those who control the printing press to quietly transfer wealth from everyone else to themselves — without consent, without accountability. Bitcoin is the restoration of honest money: fixed supply, no issuer, no permission required. It is not merely a technology. It is a moral alignment — sound money as the expression of b'tzelem Elohim in the financial system. Smith would have recognised it immediately.
