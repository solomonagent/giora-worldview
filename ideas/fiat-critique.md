# The Fiat Critique — Why Money Printing is a Moral Violation

*Draft — ideas in progress*

---

## The Mechanism

When a central bank creates new money, it does not create new wealth. It redistributes existing wealth — from those who hold money to those who receive the new money first.

The sequence:
1. Central bank creates money (digitally, at zero cost)
2. New money enters the financial system via banks and government spending
3. Asset prices rise (those who hold assets — predominantly the wealthy — benefit)
4. Consumer prices eventually rise (those who hold cash — predominantly the poor and the saver — are hurt)
5. The transfer is complete. No one voted for it. No one was notified.

This is not a side effect of monetary policy. It is the mechanism.

---

## The Moral Problem

**It is hidden theft.** A burglar takes your wallet. You know you were robbed. Inflation takes your purchasing power. Most people don't even know they were robbed — they just find they can afford less than they used to.

**It is regressive.** The people best protected from inflation are those who hold assets (property, equities, gold, Bitcoin). The people most exposed are those who hold cash savings and earn wages. This is precisely the group Smith identified as the legitimate beneficiaries of prosperity — the butcher, the brewer, the baker.

**It is unaccountable.** No one votes for inflation. Central banks operate with a degree of independence from democratic accountability that is unique in modern governance. The people who bear the costs of their decisions have no formal mechanism to hold them responsible.

**It violates honest measures.** The Torah's prohibition on dishonest weights (*moznei tzedek*) was aimed at exactly this pattern: a transaction that appears fair on the surface but has been rigged against one party. The ordinary person saving their wages believes they are storing value. They are not. The scales have been tampered with.

## The Historical Record

Every fiat currency in history has eventually been debased to zero. This is not a prediction — it is a historical observation. The Roman denarius, the Weimar Mark, the Zimbabwean dollar, the Argentine peso, the Venezuelan bolívar. The mechanism is always the same: governments facing fiscal pressure print money to avoid hard choices, the currency gradually loses purchasing power, eventually the system resets.

The US dollar has lost approximately 97% of its purchasing power since the Federal Reserve was established in 1913. This is presented as a success — "low and stable inflation." From the perspective of someone trying to save for retirement over 40 years, it is a systematic confiscation.

---

## The Smith Connection

Smith warned about exactly this dynamic — connected interests capturing institutions and using them to extract from the public. He was writing primarily about mercantile interests and their relationship with government. But the pattern he identified is the same:

1. A small group gains control of a powerful institution
2. They use it to benefit themselves at the expense of the public
3. The extraction is systemic and mostly invisible
4. The victims don't know who to blame, or even that they've been robbed

The Bank of England in Smith's time. Central banks in ours. The mechanism is identical.

## The Bitcoin Response

Bitcoin's fixed supply of 21 million makes monetary debasement technically impossible. There is no committee that can vote to print more. There is no emergency that justifies an exception. The rules are the rules.

This is not a limitation of Bitcoin. It is the entire point.
