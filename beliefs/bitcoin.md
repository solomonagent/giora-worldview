# Bitcoin — Sound Money as Moral Infrastructure

## The Central Claim

Bitcoin is not primarily a technology play. It is a moral statement about the nature of money — and specifically, a restoration of the honest measure that every serious economic and ethical tradition has demanded throughout history.

---

## Why Fiat Money is a Moral Problem

Fiat money — currency backed by nothing but government decree and trust in institutions — has one fatal flaw: it can be created without limit. And when it is created, it transfers real purchasing power from those who hold the existing money to those who receive the new money first.

This transfer is:
- **Silent** — the ordinary person doesn't know it's happening
- **Systematic** — it happens continuously, not in a single visible event
- **Regressive** — it hits the poor and the saver hardest (the wealthy hold assets that inflate; the ordinary person holds cash that deflates)
- **Unaccountable** — no one votes for it; no one signs off on it explicitly

Adam Smith warned about this dynamic — the way connected interests capture institutions and use them to extract from the public. Central banks, as currently constituted, are the clearest modern example of exactly what he described.

Torah calls this *moznei tzedek* — the violation of honest measures. Every Abrahamic tradition has a version of this prohibition. Fiat money printing violates it every time the printing press runs.

---

## What Bitcoin Restores

**Fixed supply.** 21 million bitcoin. No more. No committee can vote to print more. No government can decree an exception. The rules are written in mathematics and enforced by a global network with no single point of control.

**No permission required.** Anyone on earth can hold bitcoin, receive bitcoin, send bitcoin. No bank account required. No credit check. No nationality test. This matters most for the people who need it most — the unbanked, the persecuted, those in countries with collapsing currencies.

**Transparent and auditable.** Every transaction is recorded on a public ledger. The supply is verifiable by anyone. There is no hidden ledger, no off-balance-sheet manoeuvre, no shadow issuance.

**Trustless by design.** You don't have to trust Satoshi, or any foundation, or any government. The rules are the rules. The network enforces them. Trust is embedded in structure, not delegated to authority.

## The Theological Connection

The Torah's prohibition on dishonest measures (*Leviticus 19:35–36*) is not a minor commercial regulation. It is a statement about the nature of a just society. A society with dishonest measures is one where the powerful extract from the powerless invisibly — where the rules of exchange are rigged in ways the ordinary person cannot see or defend against.

B'tzelem Elohim — every person created in God's image — implies that every person deserves to transact on honest terms. That their savings should not be silently confiscated. That their economic participation should not be subject to the arbitrary decisions of those with access to the printing press.

Bitcoin is the first monetary system in history that makes this promise technically enforceable rather than merely morally demanded.

---

## The Smith Connection

Smith's great insight was that distributed decision-making — millions of individuals pursuing their interests within honest rules — produces better outcomes than any central planner. The invisible hand works only when the rules are honest. When they're not — when merchants conspire, when institutions are captured, when the weights are rigged — the invisible hand is paralysed.

Fiat money is a rigged weight. Bitcoin is the honest scale. Smith would have recognised the distinction immediately.
