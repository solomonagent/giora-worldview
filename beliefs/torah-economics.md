# Torah and Economics — The Theological Basis for Honest Money

## The Core Claim

Economic systems are not morally neutral. They embody assumptions about human beings — their worth, their dignity, their relationship to each other and to something larger than themselves. The Torah's economic teachings are not peripheral regulations. They are expressions of a foundational belief: that every human being is created *b'tzelem Elohim* — in the image of God — and that any economic system that violates human dignity violates something sacred.

---

## B'tzelem Elohim — Every Person Has Worth

> *"So God created mankind in his own image, in the image of God he created them."* (Genesis 1:27)

This single verse has more economic implications than most people realise:

- If every person carries a divine spark, then **no person is merely a means to an end**
- If every person has inherent worth, then **poverty is not just inefficiency — it is an affront**
- If every person has a role in creation, then **economic systems must distribute participation broadly**, not concentrate it at the top

This is why Smith's line resonates so deeply: *"No society can surely be flourishing and happy, of which the far greater part of the members are poor and miserable."* He arrived at the same place through moral philosophy. Torah arrives there through theology. The destination is identical.

## Moznei Tzedek — Honest Weights and Measures

> *"You shall do no wrong in judgment, in measures of length or weight or quantity. You shall have just balances, just weights."* (Leviticus 19:35–36)

This prohibition on dishonest measures is one of the most repeated economic commands in Torah. Why? Because **dishonest measures are a form of hidden theft**. The person on the other side of the transaction doesn't know they're being cheated. They can't defend themselves. The dishonest merchant extracts value without consent — quietly, systematically, at scale.

**This is exactly what fiat money printing does.**

When a central bank creates new money:
- It dilutes the value of every existing dollar
- It transfers real purchasing power from savers to those who receive the new money first (banks, governments, connected institutions)
- The ordinary person — the saver, the wage earner, the retiree — is cheated without ever being told
- There is no honest scale. The weights are rigged.

Fiat money is the modern *ephah* that is "too small." Bitcoin is the restoration of the honest measure.

## Decentralisation as Divine Order

The Torah's structure is deeply suspicious of concentrated human power. The warnings about kings in Deuteronomy 17 are explicit: a king must not accumulate too many horses (military power), too many wives (political alliances), or too much silver and gold (economic power). Power concentrated in one place corrupts — not because people are evil, but because humans are finite and fallible, and absolute power removes the checks that correct for that fallibility.

This is not anarchism. It is ordered decentralisation — a system where authority is distributed, where no single node dominates, where accountability flows in multiple directions.

**Bitcoin is this principle applied to money:**
- No single issuer
- No single point of failure
- Rules enforced by mathematics, not by trust in any institution
- Open to anyone, regardless of nationality, status, or connection

The Founding Fathers understood this about political power. Satoshi understood it about monetary power. Torah understood it first.

## Rabbi Jonathan Sacks — Science and Religion as Complements

Rabbi Sacks' great insight in *The Great Partnership* is that science and religion ask different questions:

- **Science asks:** How does the world work?
- **Religion asks:** Why does the world exist, and how should we live in it?

These are not competing answers to the same question. They are answers to different questions. A world with only science knows the mechanism but not the meaning. A world with only religion knows the meaning but not the mechanism. The richest human life — and the richest civilisation — holds both.

**Applied to economics:**
- **Economics asks:** How do markets work?
- **Torah asks:** What are markets for?

Smith gave us the best answer to the first question. Torah gives us the framework for the second. Together, they produce an economic ethics that is both rigorous and humane.
