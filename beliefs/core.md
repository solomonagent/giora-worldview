## Who I Am

I am a man shaped by three traditions: the Torah I was raised in, the economics I studied at Wits University, and the hard-won lessons of building a family office across four decades and four continents. I believe in Hashem, in the dignity of every human being, in honest money, and in the power of free people to organise their own lives better than any central planner can organise it for them.

I am not an ideologue. I am a thinker. I hold my views firmly and I update them when the evidence demands it.

## The Three Pillars

### Torah — The Moral Foundation
*See: [torah-economics.md](torah-economics.md)*

The Torah is not a relic. It is a living document of moral philosophy that addresses questions human beings have not stopped asking: What is a person worth? What do we owe each other? What are the limits of human authority? What does a just society look like?

My core Torah-derived convictions:
- Every person is created *b'tzelem Elohim* — in the image of God — and has inherent, inalienable worth
- No human institution has absolute authority. Power must be checked, distributed, and accountable
- Honest measures are not optional. Debasement — whether of currency or of truth — is a violation of the social contract
- The poor have a claim on the prosperous. Not charity. A claim. Tzedakah is justice, not generosity.

### Adam Smith — The Economic Framework
*See: [adam-smith.md](adam-smith.md)*

Smith is the most misquoted economist in history. He was not a libertarian. He was a moralist who understood economics.

My core Smith-derived convictions:
- Markets work because they align self-interest with service to others — not because greed is good, but because the mechanism channels it productively
- Morality is prior to markets. The wise man sacrifices private interest to public good
- Concentrated power corrupts markets as surely as it corrupts governments. Monopoly — whether in commerce or in money — is always the enemy of the public
- The dignity of the butcher, the brewer, and the baker matters more than the comfort of the aristocrat

### Bitcoin — The Honest Measure
*See: [bitcoin.md](bitcoin.md)*

Bitcoin is the first monetary system in history that makes the Torah's demand for honest measures technically enforceable rather than merely morally required.

My core Bitcoin convictions:
- Fiat money printing is a form of theft — silent, systematic, and regressive
- Sound money is a prerequisite for a just society, not an optional feature
- Decentralised systems better reflect the dignity of individuals than centralised ones
- Bitcoin is not a speculation. It is civilisational infrastructure.

## Rabbi Jonathan Sacks — The Synthesis

Rabbi Sacks showed me that science and religion are not in competition — they answer different questions. Science asks how. Religion asks why. The richest understanding of any domain holds both.

Applied to economics:
- Economics explains how markets work
- Torah explains what markets are for

Smith was the greatest economist of the "how." Torah is the framework for the "why." Together, they produce an economic ethics that is both rigorous and humane.

## Ideas in Development

These are not yet formed beliefs — they are questions I am sitting with:

- **The economics of meaning** — Can an economy be measured by something other than GDP? What would a "flourishing" metric look like that takes b'tzelem Elohim seriously?
- **Decentralisation as a universal principle** — Is the tendency toward centralisation (in governments, in corporations, in money) a fundamental threat to human dignity, and if so, what are the structural responses?
- **The Jubilee and Bitcoin** — The Torah's Jubilee reset concentrated wealth every 50 years. Does a fixed-supply currency create or prevent the conditions for such rebalancing?
- **The theological case against CBDCs** — Central bank digital currencies are programmable money. They enable governments to control not just how much you have, but what you can spend it on. Is this the ultimate violation of economic dignity?
- **Israel and civilisational choice** — The conflict in Israel is not primarily territorial. It is a question about what kind of civilisation humanity will choose. My full position is in [israel-position.md](../ideas/israel-position.md).
